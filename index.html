How to use this

Pick a mood, not a town.

The old version explained the lake by shore and village. That is useful for planning housing, but not for people arriving fresh who just want to know: what are we doing, how intense is it, and why should I care?

So this version is organized by activity. Each card explains the thing in normal English, translates the French terms, and links out to official or useful resources.

Short version: swim most days, bike when the legs want drama, eat constantly, and reserve one or two big mountain-adventure days.

Color scheme

I’d keep the palette calm and place-specific: lake blue, sage green, warm stone, terracotta, and late-afternoon gold. It feels more like Annecy itself and less like a generic travel app.

Lay of the land

The lake towns, in human terms.

Think of the lake as two useful shores: the easier bike-path west side, and the dramatic east side with prettier villages, cliffs, coves, and mountain access.

How to picture it

Annecy sits at the north end. Going clockwise from town, the east side is Veyrier, Menthon, and Talloires. Coming back up the west side, you hit Duingt, Saint-Jorioz, and Sévrier.

East shore Veyrier, Menthon, Talloires. More dramatic, more cliffy, more “wow, we are in the Alps.”
West shore Duingt, Saint-Jorioz, Sévrier. Flatter, easier by bike, great for low-friction beach days.
Getting around Line 50A serves Annecy, Sévrier, Saint-Jorioz, and Duingt. Line 60 serves Annecy, Veyrier-du-Lac, Menthon-Saint-Bernard, and Talloires-Montmin.
North end Old town Easy default

Annecy and Vieux Annecy

This is the anchor: canals, old stone streets, markets, lakefront walks, gelato, restaurants, and the place everyone can understand immediately.

Translation/context: Vieux Annecy or vieille ville means old Annecy or old town. This is where we go when we want beauty without logistics.

East shore Close to Annecy

Veyrier-du-Lac

The first east-shore village after Annecy. It feels residential, polished, and very lake-housey, with quick access to the water and views back toward town.

Good for: a softer east-shore beach stop, a scenic roll-through, or the beginning of the “pretty side of the lake” feeling.

East shore Castle energy

Menthon-Saint-Bernard

Menthon is the storybook one: shaded beach, castle above town, and an easy “we swam, then saw something historic” day.

Translation/context: château means castle. The Château de Menthon is the fairytale-looking one above the lake.

East shore Magic beaches Adventure base

Talloires and the Angon road beaches

This is the dreamy one. Talloires has the bay, the classic postcard views, swimming spots, paragliding access, and the road toward Angon where the little beaches and coves get stupidly magical.

Liv note: this is where the “secret” rope-swing energy belongs. We can keep the actual spot off the internet and just let the brothers discover it properly.

Southwest bend Photo stop

Duingt

Duingt is the little village on the bend with the château-on-the-water silhouette. It is one of the best “wait, this place is insane” moments on the loop.

Good for: beach stop, coffee/bike-path pause, old train tunnel moment, and looking back across the lake toward Talloires.

West shore Beach day

Saint-Jorioz

Saint-Jorioz is practical and beach-forward: roomy swimming area, shade, facilities, and easy access from the west-side bike path.

Good for: the easiest group beach day, a casual bike stop, and lower-drama lake time when nobody wants to overplan.

West shore Near Annecy

Sévrier

Sévrier is the close west-shore option: convenient from Annecy, easy on the bike path, and useful when we want lake access without committing to a whole expedition.

Translation/context: that accent in Sévrier does not change the plan. It is basically “Seh-vree-ay,” and it is very much the practical side of the lake.

Easy Water Daily default

Lake swims and beach afternoons

This is the baseline activity: walk or bike to the lake, swim, read, snack, repeat. Lake Annecy is unusually clear and very swim-friendly in summer.

Translation/context: plage means beach. It may be grass, pebbles, or sand, not necessarily an ocean-style beach.

Good for everyone. Low friction. Excellent for jet lag, recovery days, or “we did too much yesterday” days.

Easy to medium Bike

The lake loop by bike

This is the “everyone should do it once” ride: a scenic loop around the lake with lots of protected bike path, swim stops, and snack options.

Translation/context: voie verte means greenway: a bike/pedestrian path separated from cars.

For non-cyclists, this can be rented e-bike territory. For cyclists, it is the soft opening before the actual climbs.

Medium to spicy Bike

Climb a col

A col is a mountain pass: basically, the road climbs up and over a saddle between peaks. In cycling terms, this is where Tour de France drama happens.

Beginner translation: “Do a col” means “ride uphill for a long time, enjoy a ridiculous view, then descend.”

We can pick the level: gentle scenic climb, Forclaz day, or a full “why are we like this” alpine route.

Spicy Mountain

Via ferrata, without making everyone Google it

Via ferrata literally means “iron way.” It is a protected climbing route with metal rungs, cables, ladders, and sometimes bridges.

Normal-person explanation: It is halfway between hiking and rock climbing. You wear a harness and clip into safety cables, so it feels adventurous without requiring climbing experience.

Good if people want a memorable “we did a mountain thing” story. Bad if anyone hates exposure or heights.

Medium to spicy Water

Canyoning at Angon

Canyoning means moving down a mountain stream using a mix of walking, sliding, jumping, swimming, and rope-assisted descents. Angon is the classic nearby canyon.

Translation/context: canyon d’Angon means Angon Canyon. cascade means waterfall.

This is the “cold water, helmets, screaming in a fun way” activity. Best booked with a guide.

Spicy but guided Mountain

Paragliding from Col de la Forclaz

This is the iconic Annecy postcard activity: tandem flight above the lake, taking off from a mountain pass and landing down near the water.

Translation/context: parapente means paragliding. baptême often means first-time tandem flight, not a religious event.

Best for someone who wants the view without doing the climb. Weather-dependent and worth booking early if people are serious.

Spicy Mountain

La Tournette hike

La Tournette is the big mountain above the lake. It is gorgeous, but it is not a casual “walk after lunch.”

Plain English: Big climb, real hiking shoes, exposed sections, ladders/handrails near the top, and weather matters.

Amazing for the right group on the right day. Very skippable if people want vacation, not a character-building exercise.

Tiny glossary

French and mountain words, translated.

This is the cheat sheet for Ian and anyone who does not spend their free time learning French geography or cycling terminology.

Lac
Lake. So Lac d’Annecy simply means Lake Annecy.
Col
A mountain pass. Cyclists use it to mean a major climb over or toward a pass.
Via ferrata
“Iron way”: a protected climbing route with cables, metal rungs, ladders, or bridges.
Voie verte
Greenway: a protected bike/pedestrian path, usually separated from traffic.
Plage
Beach. Around the lake, this can mean grass, pebbles, sand, or a paid/supervised swimming area.
Baptême
For adventure sports, usually a first-time guided/tandem experience. “Baptême parapente” means a tandem paragliding flight.
Randonnée
Hike. But difficulty varies wildly, from gentle trail to “please bring real shoes.”
Cascade
Waterfall. You’ll see this around Angon and other water routes.
Vieille ville
Old town. In Annecy, this means canals, arcades, markets, restaurants, and wandering.
